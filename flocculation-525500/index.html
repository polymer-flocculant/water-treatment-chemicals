The Science behind Flocculation Flocculation is a physical-chemical process that involves the use of flocculants to destabilize particles in a liquid medium. These particles can be of various sizes and can be either organic or inorganic. The flocculants work by neutralizing the charges on the surface of the particles, causing them to come together and form larger flocs. There are two types of flocculation: coagulation and precipitation. In coagulation, the flocculants destabilize the particles and cause them to clump together. In precipitation, the flocculants form insoluble compounds with the particles, which then settle to the bottom of the liquid. The most commonly used flocculants are synthetic polymers, such as polyacrylamides, and inorganic salts, such as aluminum sulfate and ferric chloride. These chemicals are carefully selected based on the type of particles present in the liquid and the desired end result.
